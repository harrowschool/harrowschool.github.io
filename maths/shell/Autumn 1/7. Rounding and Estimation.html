Rounding and Estimation

Example: Rounding to Decimal Places

Question: Round 3.44 to 1 decimal place.

When rounding to a given number of decimal places, we are always rounding to a number with at most this number of decimal places.

. . .

In this case, that means the answer must have 1 decimal place. What are the possible answers therefore?

. . .

It has to be one of the two closest numbers with 1 decimal place: \[ 3.4 < 3.44 < 3.5 \] . . .

The first decimal place will either round up to 5 or down to 4. This is called the rounding digit.

. . .

How we decide depends on the following digit, which we call the deciding digit.

. . .

\[3.(4)\underline{4} \longrightarrow 3.4\]

Here we circle the rounding digit and underline the deciding digit. When the deciding digit is less than 5, we round down.

Example: Rounding to More Decimal Places

When we round to a particular number of decimal places we just need to circle the corresponding rounding digit, and then we deciding digit is always the one immediately following.

Round 4.650712 to 4 decimal places:

\[4.650(7)\underline{1}2 \longrightarrow 4.6507\]

Significant Figures

In a number like 27,593, each digit is worth a different amount:

  • The 2 is worth 20,000
  • The 7 is worth 7,000
  • The 5 is worth 500
  • The 9 is worth 90
  • The 3 is worth 3

The first significant figure is 2, the second is 7 etc.

In a number like 4.678,

  • The 4 is worth 4
  • The 6 is worth \(\dfrac{6}{10}\)
  • The 7 is worth \(\dfrac{7}{100}\)
  • The 8 is worth \(\dfrac{8}{1000}\)

Example: Rounding to 1 Significant Figure

When rounding with siginificant figures, the way we round depends on the size of the number.

Round 647 to 1 s.f. \[ (6)\underline{4}7 \longrightarrow 600 \]

In this case, we are rounding to the nearest hundred because the first significant figure is 6, which has a place value of 600.

Round 0.27 to 1 s.f. \[ 0.(2)\underline{7} \longrightarrow 0.3 \]

In this case, we are rounding to the nearest tenth (1 d.p.) because the first significant figure is 2, which is worth 2 tenths.

Round 6,378,527 to 1 s.f. \[ (6)\underline{3}78527 \]

In this case we are rounding to the nearest one million because the first significant figure is 6, which is worth 6 million.

Example: More than One Significant Figure

Round 647 to 2 s.f. \[ 6(4)\underline{7} \longrightarrow 650 \]

Round 0.5472 to 3 s.f. \[ 0.54(7)\underline{2} \longrightarrow 0.547 \]

Note

Initial zeros do not count as significant figures because they merely show the place value of the other digits. Zeros between non-zero digits do count, however.
